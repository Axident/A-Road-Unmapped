After some runs, a shower for you and your truck isn’t enough. Not every job was going to be a simple pickup and drive. Sometimes you would need to bring an empty trailer to the site in order to get your load. This happens frequently enough, that empty trailers become a scarce commodity, cutting into your drive time and ultimately your paycheck. Experienced drivers will sometimes stash an empty behind other trailers or a warehouse for later. I had no such hiding spots, so when I needed an empty I went where my dispatcher thought there might be one. Sometimes he was right, sometimes the trailer was full of unexpected items.

The trailer he sent me to this time was full of canned peaches.

“Oh that’s where those ended up. Nice, can you do me a favor?” He asked me when I told him what I was looking at.

“Sure, what do you need me to do?” I wondered, how bad could it be?

“Before you can use this trailer for your next load, I need you to donate those peaches.” He said typing something into his computer. “There’s a food shelter not far from where you are that will take ‘em.”

The location was soon blinking on the console in my tractor. He was right, the spot wasn’t too far from here. I could probably get this emptied and still make my pickup today. I shut the doors, backed under the nose and hooked up the air and electric lines. “I hope they have a decent loading dock,” I thought to myself as I cranked up the legs. It was getting hot already and the work was making me sweat. By the smell of things, I was definitely going to need a shower when I was done with this.

The food shelter was basically a storage shed behind a church. There was definitely not a loading dock. I couldn’t even see a decent approach to get the trailer lined up. After about 15 minutes of maneuvering back and forth between the curbed islands and the cars in the parking lot I was still not ready to back up to the building.

A white haired man in a black shirt and a white collar soon came walking across the baking hot asphalt to see if I needed some help.

“I think I can eventually get lined up,” I eyeballed my next move, “this will be tricky though.”

“I can see that. Do you want me to find the owners of some of these cars?” he suggested.

“Nah,” I said over confidently, “I got this.”

“What exactly are you delivering?” He asked.

“Canned fruit,” I pointed my thumb at the trailer, “I hope you like peaches.”

“Can I see?” He didn’t seem amused.

“Oh, did my dispatcher not call you?” I said climbing down from the truck.

“Yes,” he explained, “though sometimes dispatchers are not fully aware of the quality of the delivery.”

“Oh man, OK.” I said surprised and opened the doors.

The smell had gotten much worse. The cans were mostly stacked on pallets but many had fallen over and some were near the door.

The priest grabbed a can and looked up at me, “they’re wet.” He took a look at the can and then back at me, now much more disgusted. “And they’re expired.”

“I had no idea…”  I started.

“You can tell your dispatcher we’re done taking deliveries.” He said putting the can back on the trailer and turning away.

“I’m sorry,” I called out after him, but it was too late.

He waved without looking back at me and kept walking back to his church. I closed the trailer doors and contacted my dispatcher. He gave me another location - an actual garbage dump.

Driving to the dump wasn’t that hard but on arrival they didn’t seem any fonder of a Schneider truck rolling up than that priest did.

“What’s all this then?” A chubby woman with curly hair and glasses stopped me at the gate.

“It’s canned fruit,” I started, “Did my dispatcher call you?”

“We don’t take that shit here,” she folded her arms.

“It’s spoiled, nobody will take it.” I explained, “if not here, where?”

She put her hands on her hips. “You wanna unload it yerself?”

“I can do that,” I said, not fully aware of what I was signing up for.

“We charge by the pound. You can go to the blue gate marked “C” after you get a receipt at the scale,” she pointed to the scales on the side of the driveway headed into the dump.

I cleared the cost with my dispatcher and pulled up to the scales. After a few seconds of settling, the filthy rusted machine spit out a coil of paper with a barcode on it. I assumed this was my receipt and pulled up to the intersection at the end of the driveway. The arrows pointing to the left were orange and green with marks “A” and “B” on them. The blue arrow marked “C” pointed to the right, so that's where I went.

As I approached I could see a man dragging an overloaded cart with a broken wheel out of the fenced in pit. I tried to ask him how this worked, but he just pointed to the pile of refuse inside the gate. I turned around the trailer and swung open the doors. Somehow the smell had gotten even worse.

I got back into my truck and put on my steel toe boots, an old pair of jeans and a set of heavy work gloves. I turned up my boom box and climbed into the back of the trailer. The cans weighed 6 pounds each, stacked hip high for about ⅔ the length of the trailer. This would take longer than I thought.

At first I was throwing them underhanded one at a time off the end of the trailer, but as I got deeper in I had to throw further and further. I was sweating and breathing hard and took a moment to lean against one of the poorly stacked pallets. The bottom cans had ruptured and the whole pile suddenly fell over sending dozens of cans rolling down the trailer. A light bulb went lit up over my head.

After I cleared the runway of haphazardly aligned cans and empty pallets, I created a clear runway for the remaining load of peaches and  fruit-cocktail. The cans that were still in tact rolled easy enough that I actually got pretty good at bowling with them. Many were to far gone and required a good boot to clear the end of the trailer. Once in a while, a can would be so far gone that kicking created more of a mess than anything, sending foul syrup all over the walls, my pants and shirt. By the time I finished, I was covered in the stuff but I had an empty trailer.

I closed the doors and drove back up to the entry gate. The woman at the gate had me get another reading from their old scale to compare the difference. I took the receipts back to the security shack where she managed the exit and waited for the bill. I don’t know what I was hoping would happen next, perhaps an invoice or something I could take to Shneider for them to pay. Instead, I had to pay with a personal check. I missed my pickup, insulted a priest, got half a garbage dump in my clothes and hair, and it was going to cost me money - nice.

Schneider did end up paying me back of course, but I had to throw those clothes and gloves away. To this day, whenever anyone uses the phrase “hot garbage” I smell expired peaches.
