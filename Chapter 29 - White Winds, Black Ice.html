It was snowing pretty hard by the time I got to Harrisburg. The handoff location was a major center of operations for Schneider. It was easy to get to, the road was plowed and there were plenty of spaces for my trailer. I fueled up at the Schneider pump and went inside for a hot meal and a shower. By the time I came out, it had gotten dark and the snow storm had gotten worse. I trudged over some snow drifts on my way to my rig, where there was a message waiting for me from my dispatcher.

I had a load ready for me in the morning, but I’d need to go and fetch my own empty trailer from behind a nearby department store. I flipped over the cassette in my boombox and pushed in the air brake release. If I was going to get this trailer before I got snowed in, I’d need to go right now.

Driving bobtail in a cabover style tractor like I was in meant that you’d fit just inside the same space as any large SUV. The truck could just barely fit in a parking spot, corners could be made without swinging wide, but the best part was you could accelerate like a normal car. All the lower gears for building momentum while pulling a heavy load were useless. The rig could start right in 7th and pull away from a stoplight with a surprising amount of speed. Of course, this only works when you’ve got enough traction to keep your tires from slipping.

I did not.

When enough snow and ice has been pounded into a slick glossy mirror across the road, you have an incredibly slippery glaze left on the asphalt often referred to as “black ice”. It blends in with the rest of the road and offers no traction whatsoever. A puck sliding across an air-hockey table has a better chance of slowing down or changing direction than a car driving on black ice.

The wet course training would come in handy tonight. On my first turn my drive tires slid sideways sending me on an awkward course through the empty intersection. I shoved in the clutch and let the wheels naturally settle to match the speed of the passing street below them. I maintained the aim of my steer tires at my intended direction, and when the drive tires connected with the road again I was on my way.

I was much more careful at the next two intersections, but the long arc in the road leading to the department store took me by surprise. I felt the tires lose their grip, but this time the clutch trick had no effect. The steering wheel was also useless. I was sliding sideways on black ice at about 25 mph sitting on 9 tons of steel and diesel fuel. The road banked slightly as it rounded the bend, just enough to keep me on the road. I was separated from the oncoming lanes by a boulevard of bushes and streetlights clouded by steadily falling oversized snowflakes. I watched each pole and bush pass quietly to my left under the headlights of my truck as I slid the entire length of the block without hitting a thing.

I finally came to rest in the middle of the next intersection, facing exactly the direction I needed to continue. I looked both ways, shifted into a lower gear and slowly drove around the back of the store. The empty trailer was nowhere to be found.

I pulled into the parking lot and messaged my dispatcher, explaining that the trailer simply wasn’t where it was supposed to be. It was a long time before another trailer number came up on my screen. By then there was just too much snow on the road to safely go hunting for a trailer. I sent another message saying that given the weather and driving conditions it just wasn’t safe to continue. I released the brakes and carefully headed back to the Schneider lot.
