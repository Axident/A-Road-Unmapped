The next morning was much colder. Salt trucks and plows had been out all night long, making sure the streets were clear but nothing was going to fix the temperature. Luckily I’d left my truck running all night, so starting it wasn’t going to be an issue. A blinking light on my dashboard computer let me know my dispatcher was already up.

Apparently, the load I was going to need that empty for was already gone. A new load was ready, right here in the yard. I went inside briefly to freshen up and grab a cup of coffee before hooking up to the trailer. It was easy enough to find, and looked in perfect shape. I backed my fifth wheel under the kingpin and hopped out to connect the air and electric lines. Out of habit, I pushed on the release handle to make sure my load was secure and heard it snap into place.

I couldn’t believe that just happened. The handle should definitely not needed my help to snap into place, backing into the kingpin should’ve done that already. This was absolutely a safety hazard. I hopped down from my rig and went to the garage to see if someone could look at it.

“You bet,” a tall skinny man with a ball cap said, “pull into bay 3 and we’ll have someone check it out.”

I unhooked the trailer and pulled my tractor into bay 3. I tried to find someone to explain what was going on, but the only 2 guys in the shop were clearly busy. I shut down the engine and went inside to watch the weather channel.

This was a pretty nice place to be stuck, if you had to be stuck somewhere. There was a comfortable lounge, clean showers, and this morning they had a breakfast buffet. I sat and ate pancakes while I watched the storm tracker and some coverage of a car accident on the freeway.

About 2 and ½  hours later, someone finally came out from the garage to tell me what was going on with my truck.

“We topped off your air pressure and filled up your washer fluid,” the guy said, “but there’s nothing wrong with that pull handle.”

“Seriously,” I couldn’t believe it, “nothing at all?”

“Nope, you’re in good shape.” The guy smiled and handed me the keys.  “Your tractor is out front,” he said and turned to walk back to the garage. As he opened the door I expected the cold outside air to rush in, but no air rushed in at all.

I walked over to the window in the door and looked into the garage. All the bay doors were shut. I opened the door to ask if I could talk to whoever looked at my truck and noticed the air was actually warm. The garage was heated.  My truck had sat in a heated garage for hours before anyone looked at it. My faith that “I was in good shape” went right in the trash bin.

I found my rig outside, still running, and backed up to my trailer once again. This time I triple checked the handle to make sure I was securely in place. Satisfied with the connection, I hopped back in the truck and planned my route. I was headed to the middle of nowhere, Illinois.
